What is Blockchain?

Understanding the foundation of decentralized technology and why it matters.

The Problem with Centralization

Traditional internet services rely on centralized servers. When you send a message, make a payment, or store a file, your data flows through servers owned by companies like Google, Amazon, or banks.

This creates several problems:

  • Single point of failure - If the server goes down, the service stops
  • Trust requirement - You must trust the company to handle your data honestly
  • Censorship risk - The company can deny you access at any time
  • Data vulnerability - Centralized databases are prime targets for hackers

In a peer-to-peer network, every participant can communicate with every other participant directly. There's no central authority that can shut down the network or censor transactions.

What Makes a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a specific type of peer-to-peer network with these properties:

Distributed Ledger

Every participant holds a complete copy of all transaction history. No single entity controls the data.

Immutability

Once data is written, it cannot be changed or deleted. Each block links cryptographically to the previous one.

Consensus

Network participants agree on the state of the ledger through mathematical rules, not trust in any authority.

Transparency

All transactions are publicly visible and verifiable by anyone. The rules are open source code.

How Consensus Works

The magic of blockchain is getting thousands of computers to agree on the same truth without trusting each other. This is called consensus.

Different blockchains use different consensus mechanisms:

  • Proof of Work (Bitcoin) - Miners compete to solve mathematical puzzles. First to solve gets to add the next block.
  • Proof of Stake (Ethereum) - Validators stake tokens as collateral. Bad behavior means losing their stake.
  • Proof of History (Solana) - A cryptographic clock provides a trusted source of time, enabling parallel processing.
Why This Matters

Proof of History is Solana's key innovation. By establishing a trusted timeline before consensus, Solana can process thousands of transactions in parallel while other blockchains must process them one at a time.

The Blockchain Trilemma

Every blockchain must balance three competing properties:

  • Security - Resistance to attacks and fraud
  • Decentralization - No single point of control
  • Scalability - Ability to handle many transactions

Traditional blockchains like Bitcoin prioritize security and decentralization, but sacrifice scalability (only ~7 transactions per second). Solana's architecture aims to maximize all three.

Summary

  • Blockchains are peer-to-peer networks with no central authority
  • Data is stored in linked blocks that cannot be changed
  • Consensus mechanisms allow agreement without trust
  • Different blockchains make different tradeoffs
  • Solana uses Proof of History for high performance
